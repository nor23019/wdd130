Stoicism

Stoicism is a philosophical school of thought that originated in ancient Greece, primarily in Athens, around the 3rd century BC. It was founded by Zeno of Citium and later developed by philosophers such as Epictetus, Seneca the Younger, and Marcus Aurelius. Key tenets of Stoicism include virtue as the highest good, acceptance of what is beyond our control, living in accordance with nature, practicing mindfulness and self-awareness, and fostering endurance and resilience in the face of adversity.

Cardinal Virtues

Wisdom

Wisdom encompasses both practical and theoretical knowledge, including understanding the fundamental principles of nature and living in accordance with reason. It involves making sound judgments, seeking truth, and developing a deep understanding of oneself and the world.

Temperance

Temperance, also known as self-control or moderation, is the ability to govern one's desires, impulses, and appetites in pursuit of a balanced and virtuous life. It involves practicing restraint, moderation, and discipline in all aspects of life, avoiding excess and indulgence.

Justice

Justice is the moral virtue of treating others with fairness, equity, and respect. It involves fulfilling one's duties and obligations to society, upholding moral principles, and promoting harmony and cooperation among individuals.

Courage

Courage involves facing challenges, adversity, and fears with bravery and resilience. It is the ability to act virtuously despite obstacles, discomfort, or danger, and to maintain one's convictions and principles in the face of opposition.
